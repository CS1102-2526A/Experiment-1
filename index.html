Observing a Neutralisation Reaction with Litmus

Let's explore a classic acid-base reaction between hydrochloric acid (HCl) and sodium hydroxide (NaOH). When HCl and NaOH are combined, these two chemicals react in a neutralisation reaction and form sodium chloride (NaCl), which is commonly defined as table salt, and water (H₂O). The chemical equation is:

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

What is neutralisation?

When there is an exact amount of both alkali and acid mixed, they will cancel each other out and form a neutral solution. The reaction is called neutralisation.

But how can we observe these chemical changes obviously? Since the results, like colours, of acid-base reaction are often clear, turbid or colourless, we can use a pH indicator to examine the chemical changes.


Using Litmus as an Indicator

One of the pH indicators commonly used is Litmus Paper, which is made from a dye, its colour will change based on whether a solution is acidic or alkaline.

Litmus paper colour will turns red if there is acidic solutions, turns blue in alkaline solutions, and stay constant of what colour of litmus paper is using in neutral solutions.
